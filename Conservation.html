Conservation birds

Minnesota has several bird species that are in need of conservation and management, including the Greater Prairie Chicken, Sharp-tailed Grouse, Henslow's Sparrow, Upland Sandpiper, Piping Plover, and various waterfowl such as the threatened Trumpeter Swan and endangered Whooping Crane.

Greater Prairie-Chicken

The Greater Prairie-Chicken is a ground-dwelling bird that lives in tallgrass prairies in western and central Minnesota. They are known for their distinctive courtship display, where the males gather in groups to inflate their bright orange air sacs and make booming calls to attract females. Their populations have declined due to habitat loss and fragmentation, but efforts are being made to conserve their remaining habitat and restore degraded prairies.

Henslow's Sparrow

Henslow's Sparrow is a small bird that lives in grasslands and prairies in southeastern Minnesota. They have a distinctive song that sounds like a insect buzzing, and are often difficult to spot due to their secretive nature. Their populations have declined due to habitat loss and degradation, and efforts are being made to restore and manage grasslands for their benefit.

Kirtland's Warbler

TKirtland's Warbler is a rare and endangered bird that breeds in jack pine forests in northern Michigan and winters in the Bahamas. Minnesota is a key stopover site for the species during migration, and efforts are being made to manage and restore suitable habitat for their benefit.

Piping Plover

Piping Plover is a small, sandy-colored bird that breeds on the beaches of the Great Lakes and winters along the Gulf of Mexico. Their populations have declined due to habitat loss and disturbance, and efforts are being made to protect their breeding habitat and educate the public about their conservation.

American Bittern

American Bittern is a medium-sized wading bird that lives in wetlands throughout Minnesota. They have a distinctive camouflage pattern that allows them to blend in with the surrounding reeds and grasses. Their populations have declined due to habitat loss and degradation, and efforts are being made to conserve and restore wetland habitat for their benefit.

Yellow Rail

The Yellow Rail is a small bird that lives in marshes and wetlands in northern Minnesota. They have a distinctive call that sounds like two small stones being tapped together, and are often difficult to spot due to their secretive nature. Their populations have declined due to habitat loss and degradation, and efforts are being made to conserve and restore wetland habitat for their benefit.

Connecticut Warbler

Connecticut Warbler is a small, brightly colored bird that breeds in the boreal forests of northern Minnesota. They are difficult to spot due to their secretive nature and are often only detected by their distinctive call. Their populations have declined due to habitat loss and fragmentation, and efforts are being made to conserve and manage suitable habitat for their benefit.
